When I was in middle school, my parents got me a big [National Geographic map](https://www.natgeomaps.com/re-world-executive-poster-size). The Earth was displayed on it in three different projections: the [Winkel-Tripel]({% link _projections/winkel-tripel.md %}) serving as the centerpiece, with [azimuthal equidistant]({% link _projections/azimuthal-equidistant.md %}) aspects showcasing the poles and a couple of [Goode homolosine]({% link _projections/goode-homolosine.md %}) 'orange-peels' demonstrating some Earth-wide data. I had a globe as well, but the flat map was much larger, showed the Earth and its nations all at once from a distance, and had much better detail when I walked up close. Because the main projection was Winkel-Tripel, rather than Mercator or, God forbid, [Gall-Peters]({% link _projections/cylindrical-equal-area.md %}), I had a better sense of the northern hemisphere's scale and shape compared to the rest of the Earth at a glance.

I didn't realize it at the time, but this was my first experience with the value different map projections provide. Having three different projections in the same scene motivated curiosity about different ways to display the Earth. That interest has simmered for many years as I acquired more maps and knowledge about them. Starting today, I hope to satisfy and inspire that curiosity in others with the launch of Flatsphere: a tool to easily manipulate, transform, and export images in a wide variety of projections.

## Why Projections?

We can't flatten a sphere or ellipsoid without introducing distortion somewhere, and this mathematical impossibility drives the need for different types of projections. If that were the only issue, we might have settled on a few standard projections centuries ago and been done with it. However, projection diversity is further driven by trying to maintain certain useful properties in a map, often at the expense of other properties. Consider *conformality*, a property of projections that preserve local angles and shapes. Both the [Mercator]({% link _projections/mercator.md %}) and [Lagrange]({% link _projections/lagrange.md %}) projections are conformal, yet look vastly different. Mercator escapes the impossibility constraint by sending the poles to infinity; Lagrange fits the whole sphere inside a finite circle with both poles pinched to singular points on its edge. Projections that strictly keep to one or more such properties usually have some type of mathematical oddity shadowing their extremities. Trying to avoid those oddities leads us into the whole separate category of compromise projections (like the Winkel-Tripel from my childhood map) that blend a few formal properties without strictly keeping to any one.

## The Path to Flatsphere

I finally began to explore projections more in depth a couple of years ago, when I created a [library](https://github.com/glossopoeia/flatsphere) in the Go programming language for general-purpose forward and inverse projection. This was motivated in part by the excellent work done by [Justin Kunimune](https://kunimune.blog/) on his [Map Projection software](https://github.com/jkunimune/Map-Projections), and also by what I saw as the lack of Go-native projection solutions. My initial interest was to understand how the [HEALPix]({% link _projections/healpix.md %}) projection worked and use it for some digital elevation datasets, but I fell into the rabbit hole of projection math thanks to his elegant implementation. I worked on the Go library for a while and used it for some side projects, but I was satisfied with working on the numerical mathematics side and not porting Kunimune's full visualization suite to Go.

It turns out that projection math is the perfect fit for GPUs. Because each projection can be computed independently from its latitude and longitude inputs (needing no other information from its neighbors), it's a classic example of an "embarrassingly parallel" problem of the sort GPUs eat for breakfast. Last year I got the itch to learn WebGPU, as that API recently gained wide support. To get a more bare metal understanding, I needed a project I could work on to really understand how the mechanics all worked together. Since I already had the Flatsphere library fairly built out and moderately tested, I would be able to focus on learning about the GPU layer rather than learning some other domain in parallel. These three threads — my continued interest in projections, the Go library where I cut my teeth on the math, and the WebGPU project that gave me a runtime worth using — converged into the Flatsphere I'm launching today.

## Flatsphere Today

So what can we do with Flatsphere right now? The most obvious is to see the Earth in over 15 different projections. But Flatsphere also renders more than just the default Earth scene. Suppose you have an [equirectangular image of Mars](https://www.solarsystemscope.com/textures/download/2k_mars.jpg) you want to turn into a view from space using the [orthographic]({% link _projections/orthographic.md %}) projection: paste a link or upload your image, set the source projection, then choose orthographic. You can then drag your mouse to transform the view at high resolution and interactive framerates thanks to the WebGPU rendering pipeline. You're also not limited to transformations of planetary bodies: the [background radiation of the universe](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/WMAP_2012.png) and other whole-sky data can be supplied as input and reprojected the same as any other projection source. If you come up with something you want to save, an export will automatically include the projection information in the image metadata for you.

## Key Takeaways from the Journey

A few things really stuck out to me while building Flatsphere. Most obvious in hindsight was how much having a visualization helps when debugging what is, at its core, a visualization problem. The Go library had some hard-coded numbers and a suite of property tests prior to the WebGPU pipelines, but visualizing the projections in Flatsphere immediately and viscerally pointed to problems with a few of the projections that the property tests and simple hardcoded cases had missed. The Aitoff and Hammer projection discrepancies were particularly bad.

The most serendipitous finding was stumbling upon [WESL](https://github.com/webgpu-tools/wesl-spec) while looking for fragment shader resources. For all its cool capabilities, WGSL as a language is terrible for 'Don't Repeat Yourself' and code abstraction. WESL enabled me to factor out a common interface that all projections could implement, which also helped make each pipeline more efficient by removing an n²-case branch (GPUs don't like 'ifs') from the top-level of the fragment shader. WESL is a great project, and I plan to write up the full story of switching to it and what the n² dispatch looked like before and after in a separate post.

With all the floating point math involved, I expected to have far more issues with NaNs and infinities. And I did bring over a lot of the precautionary math that the Go library had already developed. Even so, in the vast majority of my testing I haven't run into problems with singularities or undefined operations where one might expect. Maybe I'm just not zoomed in far enough to get the relevant divisors to approach zero closely enough to start generating odd rendering artifacts. I'd like to do a proper investigation of where the precision actually breaks down and write up what I find.

I also learned I had accidentally implemented two different algorithms for the [Hammer]({% link _projections/hammer.md %}) projection, thinking one of them was [Aitoff]({% link _projections/aitoff.md %}). I'll share the full story in its own post soon.

## The Future of Flatsphere

In the meantime, I'm continuing to extend Flatsphere with more projections, more display tools, and more capabilities for transforming larger sets of imagery. I don't want to lock in on the current layout yet, nor chart too specific a course for the future, so expect changes to be announced rather than projected for now. The blog and projection gallery, for instance, were never part of the plan I started with. As the project grew I realized the interactive map itself wasn't capable of relaying all the information I wanted to share, so I expanded the original single-page application into a full site. Interactive cartography will remain the core of the project, as will the exploratory and intuition-building spirit of the site as a whole. And I'd like the project to grow partly in response to what people want from it.

This blog will focus on the why and the how of projections, exciting developments from the world of cartographic projections and how they apply to Flatsphere, and even some interesting historical cartographic tidbits. The goal is for the blog to provide educational value that the interactive map cannot. As I'm developing in the open, some posts will also detail how Flatsphere implements projections on the GPU, and the why and how of certain overlays or features that are included. Throughout I'll try to keep the tone approachable, but will not sacrifice technical detail where it seems necessary for clarity or precision. The overall vision of Flatsphere is a cartography-focused moving target, so give it a try and if there's a projection you wish it supported or a question you wish were answered, submit an issue on [GitHub](https://github.com/glossopoeia/flatsphereweb) and I'll take a look.
